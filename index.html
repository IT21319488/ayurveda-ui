Project Scope


Literature Survey

Ayurveda is an ancient Indian system of medicine that takes a holistic approach to health and well-being. It emphasizes maintaining balance in the body, mind, and spirit through natural remedies, diet, and lifestyle adjustments. Unlike modern medicine, which primarily focuses on treating diseases, Ayurveda places great importance on prevention and personalized care. One of its core principles is the Tridosha concept, which classifies individuals based on three biological energies—Vata, Pitta, and Kapha. These doshas determine a person’s prakurthi, or inherent constitution, which influences their physical, mental, and emotional characteristics. Understanding prakurthi is essential because it helps in tailoring diet, lifestyle, and treatments to an individual’s specific needs[1].

Determining a person’s prakurthi a complex process that requires deep knowledge of Ayurvedic principles. It involves analyzing various characteristics such as body shape, skin texture, digestion, sleep patterns, and psychological tendencies. Many beginners in Ayurveda struggle with accurate diagnosis because the assessment can be subtle and subjective. To address this challenge, we are developing an advanced tool that utilizes facial, eye, hair, and nail analysis to determine an individual's prakurthi. The primary objective of this tool is to assist Ayurvedic practitioners in making precise assessments without requiring years of experience. By combining technological advancements with traditional Ayurvedic knowledge, this innovation ensures consistency and reliability in identifying a person’s constitution, ultimately improving the accuracy of diagnosis and treatment planning.

This tool incorporates several advanced features to enhance its diagnostic capabilities. It includes micro-expression analysis to capture subtle facial expressions that provide insights into emotional tendencies. Traditional Chinese medicine techniques are also integrated to assess internal organ health and physiological functions through facial mapping. Additionally, the tool analyzes blinking patterns and eye movements to determine stress and fatigue levels. It conducts pattern-based alopecia analysis and hair texture evaluation to identify hair loss and changes in scalp health. Furthermore, it assesses cardiovascular health and blood circulation using capillary refill time analysis based on nail color, texture, and markings. These features enable a comprehensive assessment that merges modern scientific insights with traditional Ayurvedic diagnostics.

Our main goal is to bridge the gap between traditional Ayurvedic diagnostics and modern technology by leveraging image processing and deep learning techniques. By automating and standardizing the prakurthi evaluation process, this tool enhances the accuracy and accessibility of Ayurvedic diagnostics. The integration of deep learning ensures that even beginner practitioners can make well-informed decisions with greater confidence. With the growing global interest in holistic and personalized medicine, Ayurveda holds significant potential for the future. The development of this tool not only preserves the ancient wisdom of Ayurveda but also makes it more accessible and relevant in modernhealthcare settings. Through this initiative, we aim to empower the next generation of practitioners by providing them with an advanced yet easy-to-use diagnostic tool that enhances their clinical expertise.

Research Gap

Many existing studies have attempted to digitize Ayurvedic Prakriti (body constitution) classification using biometric signals, particularly focusing on wrist pulse analysis. Research by G.C. et al. and Veerabhadrappa et al. represents early efforts in this area, but these systems mainly rely on traditional signal processing and hardware-based pulse acquisition. This results in subjective interpretations, limited scalability, and lack of real-time application.

Other approaches, such as those by Gadre and Heller and Rotti et al., use questionnaires or facial image analysis to determine dosha dominance. While these reduce some subjectivity, they still depend on static, single-modality inputs and traditional machine learning methods, which fail to capture the complex, dynamic, and interconnected nature of human physiology as described in Ayurveda. Additionally, they neglect critical biometric indicators like eye movement, nail structure, or hair texture.

A major limitation in current research is the lack of multi-modal integration—studies typically analyze each feature (face, hair, nails, etc.) separately. Ayurveda emphasizes the holistic interplay between various physiological and psychological markers, and existing systems do not reflect this principle. Moreover, few solutions utilize mobile platforms, which are essential for real-time, accessible diagnostics.

Meanwhile, other biomedical studies have advanced in areas like facial micro-expression detection (Xie et al.), complexion recognition (Lin and Wang), stress tracking through eye gaze (Jyotsna and Amudha), and hair loss analysis (Sayyad et al.). However, these are used for isolated conditions and not integrated into a unified Ayurvedic framework. Nail-based capillary refill time (CRT) analysis, which offers cardiovascular insights, also remains unlinked from broader constitution assessments.

To address these gaps, this research proposes Ayur Prakruthi, a deep learning-powered mobile application that holistically analyzes multiple biometric features—face, hair, eyes, and nails—to classify an individual's prakriti. Using advanced models like CNN, ResNet, and EfficientNet, it extracts subtle physiological patterns that indicate dosha imbalances.

Unique features include:

  • Micro-expression analysis for psychological assessment.
  • Facial region mapping based on Traditional Chinese Medicine (TCM) linked to Ayurvedic principles.
  • Ocular analysis (blink rate, eye movement) to infer stress and fatigue.
  • Hair and scalp pattern recognition to detect texture and alopecia types related to dosha imbalances.
  • CRT-based nail analysis for evaluating microcirculation and cardiovascular health.

The system is implemented using a Flask backend API, with Ionic React for the mobile interface and Firebase for real-time cloud storage and data handling. Unlike past models requiring offline analysis, this mobile-based platform allows real-time, scalable, and user-friendly Prakurthi assessment, benefiting both healthcare professionals and individual users.

In conclusion, Ayur Prakruthi bridges the gap between traditional Ayurvedic diagnostics and modern AI technologies. It offers a precise, objective, and holistic solution for prakriti classification—representing a significant advancement in personalized Ayurvedic healthcare.

Research Problem & Solution

Research Problem

How can we develop an advanced diagnostic tool that enables novice Ayurvedic practitioners to accurately determine a patient's Prakrurthi by analyzing facial, ocular, hair, and nail features using image processing and deep learning techniques?

Proposed Solution

To develop an advanced diagnostic mobile app that enables novice Ayurvedic practitioners to accurately determine a patient's Prakrurthi by analyzing facial, ocular, hair, and nail features using image processing and deep learning, you can follow a structured, modular approach combining AI technology with Ayurvedic domain expertise.

Research Objectives

Main Objective

To develop a mobile app that uses image processing and deep learning to assist in the accurate diagnosis of Prakrurthi for novice Ayurvedic practitioners.

Sub Objective

To develop an advanced diagnostic tool that utilizes sophisticated image processing and deep learning techniques to accurately extract and analyze key facial features — including micro-expressions and skin texture — for precise Prakruti classification, while offering a user-friendly interface accessible to novice Ayurvedic practitioners in clinical settings.

Develop an automated Prakruti identification system using comprehensive eye analysis—including iris color, sclera condition, eye size, eyebrow structure, blinking rates, and eye movement patterns—to assess stress, fatigue, and dosha imbalances, integrated into a user-friendly mobile app for seamless data collection, analysis, and result presentation.

To build a robust hair analysis module by curating a comprehensive dataset representing diverse hair types and conditions, applying advanced image processing to extract features like texture, thickness, color, and scalp health, and integrating novel diagnostic indicators such as patterned alopecia and detailed texture analysis.

To develop a precise nail analysis tool for Prakruti diagnosis by applying advanced image processing to extract key nail characteristics—including color, texture, shape, and markings—integrating unique diagnostic features such as Capillary Refill Time (CRT) and abnormality detection, training machine learning models for accurate classification, and designing a user-friendly interface for novice Ayurvedic practitioners in clinical use.

Methodology


This research focuses on developing an advanced diagnostic tool that integrates traditional Ayurvedic principles with modern image processing and deep learning to accurately determine an individual's prakurthi. Data collection was conducted in Ayurvedic hospitals with informed consent, capturing images of four key features—face, eyes, hair, and nails—that were identified as essential for prakurthi analysis through consultation with experienced practitioners. The collected images underwent preprocessing steps such as contrast enhancement, noise reduction, and data augmentation techniques like rotation, flipping, cropping, and brightness adjustments to enhance data quality and model robustness. Facial images, taken from multiple angles, were analyzed using convolutional neural networks (CNN) to extract structural features, skin texture, and subtle micro-expressions. Eye analysis involved detection of iris color, blinking patterns, and movements to provide insights into physiological and emotional conditions. Hair images were processed to evaluate texture, scalp health, and alopecia patterns, while nail images were examined for color, texture, and capillary refill time indicators related to cardiovascular health. Expert Ayurvedic doctors labeled the dataset according to the Tridosha prakurthi classification, including Vata, Pitta, Kapha, and their combinations. The CNN models were trained on these labeled images, and the system combined predictions from all four components to determine the final prakurthi classification. In situations where results were inconclusive or tied, the system incorporated a supplementary questionnaire to refine the diagnosis based on self-reported Ayurvedic attributes. The tool’s performance was validated on separate data subsets, assessing accuracy and reliability through standard classification metrics and receiving practical feedback from Ayurvedic practitioners using the system in clinical environments. This methodology bridges ancient Ayurvedic knowledge with contemporary AI techniques to create a standardized, objective, and accessible prakurthi diagnostic tool, ultimately enhancing personalized healthcare and practitioner confidence.
